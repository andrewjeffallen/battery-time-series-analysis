Question 1.2:¶

For the PV meter, backfill the power and energy data, prove that dEnergy/dt matches power, and justify that your approach is physically reasonable.

To answer this question, I used the equation:

dEnergy/dt = “d[pv_pos_ltea_kwh] / dt” = pv_p_kw

The backfill method interpolation using scipy.interpolate method. I used time to interpolate given length of time interval.

dEnergy/dt , represented by d_pv_energy_dt, was calculated by taking the rate of change of pv_pos_ltea_kwh and converting to kws to kwh.

After dEnergy/dt for PV was calculated, I applied a rolling average to dEnergy/dt , using a 30 second window (30 intervals given 1 second intervals).

From the graph below, you can see this approach is reasonable as dEnergy/dt approximates pv_p_kw

In [14]:
df_backfill = df_reindexed.interpolate(method='time',inplace=False)


df_backfill['d_pv_energy_dt'] = df_backfill.pv_pos_ltea_kwh.diff()*3600
df_backfill['d_pv_energy_dt_ma'] = df_backfill.d_pv_energy_dt.rolling(window=30).mean()


plt.plot(df_backfill.d_pv_energy_dt_ma,label='dEnergy/dt',alpha=.5,linewidth=3)
plt.plot(df_backfill.pv_p_kw,label='Backfilled PV Power')
plt.title("PV Power over time")
plt.legend(loc='upper right')
plt.show()

Question 1.3:¶

For the ESS meter, backfill the power and energy data, prove that dEnergy/dt matches power, and justify your choice of backfill method.

To answer this question, I used a similar backfill method, using the scipy.interpolate method.

dEnergy/dt , represented by d_ba_energy_dt, was calculated by taking the rate of change of ba_neg_ltea_kwh if the power was negative & the rate of change of ba_neg_ltea_kwh if the power was positive. Lastly, kw(seconds) was converted to kw(hours).

This backfill method is reasonable as show in the plots of dEnergy/dt and power over time. They are roughly equivalent.

After the data was backfilled, the mean net load was calculated 2 ways

  2. Rolling Average of ut_p_kw with window of 15 minutes (15 seconds * 60 minutes/seconds)

Question 2.3¶

What impact does data backfill method have on estimated demand charges for this site?

The data backfill method can affect the bias of the predicted values. A reliable backfilling method is needed to reduce risk of long unmonitored gaps in the data, and could help make decisions on assessing performance of the system..

In the ideal scenario, if adequate data supporting the equation gross load = net load + PV + ESS were known, then a good backfill method would be to apply the equation across missing values. However, ideal scenarios never happen.

A common way to handle missing values is dropping values, in this context, dropping values would lower the actual cost of estimated demand charge as those data points would not be considered in the calculation. Some sort of backfill is required.

The time backfill method was selected because because the whole table was interpolated as a two-dimensional plane instead of as separate single-dimensional series. If the time index was evenly spaced, then linear interpolation would also be appropriate, but this was not the case.

In the context of this data, a more simple backfill method (linear/time interpolation) would give more conservative predictions, than a more complex backfill (deep neural nets, KNN, etc.) method. One downside of simple backfill methods is the case of extreme values. This could lead to very high or low estimated demand charges for the site. One upside of a complex backfill method is possible accuracy gain in the case extreme values.
